About the Project

Starting in the 1990s, restrictive immigration policies in European countries changed migration flows and made the Americas more attractive for African immigrants. Statistics show that the number of African immigrants in the Americas has grown rapidly. In the last 15 years, Africans have shown the fastest growth rate among Black immigrant groups in the US. In Canada, Africa now ranks second as a source continent of recent immigrants. In Brazil, Africans represent 65 percent of all asylum seekers, according to Brazil’s National Committee for Refugees (Conare). Many of Africa’s most acclaimed writers such as Wole Soyinka, Chinua Achebe, and Chimamanda N. Adichie have lived in the Americas in order to escape persecution, pursue higher education, or benefit from job mobility. As such, the themes of exile and migration often find their way into their work, resulting in a blend of biographical and fictional itineraries.

Considering migration to the Americas as an important theme in contemporary African fiction, this digital StoryMap aims to explore and visualize transatlantic migration in African literature through a computing and cartographic approach with the goal of mapping and performing distant readings of texts that deal with migration to Brazil, Canada, and the US – the three most prominent destinations in African literature. The texts featured in this map are written by authors from across the African continent and published between 1990 and 2022 in English, French, Portuguese, and Arabic, thus overriding the limiting compartmentalization of African Studies divided by language.

By mapping geographic and sociological data collected from a large number of novels, this StoryMap will reveal the multi-sited nature of African writing produced by flows and circulations as well as the multidimensional experience of migration – voluntary or involuntary; direct or indirect. In doing this, this map will contribute to the decolonization of the Digital Humanities (DH) by applying its tools to African Studies.

The Team

Abena Somiah

Abena Somiah is a Ghanaian MA student at UBC's Department of Geography. Her research explores the migration of sub-Saharan Africans to Japan, and how they negotiate identity, community and belonging.

Carsim Birmingham

Carsim Birmingham is an Undergraduate 4th year French Major at UBC from a small island in the Caribbean named the Commonwealth of Dominica. He is an aspiring singer and Creolist.

Fabiola del Rincón Fernández

Fabiola is a second-year doctorate student at UBC. Her research examines the conceptualization of memory in the literary/artistic work of contemporary female authors from Latin America and the Caribbean.

Arnaud Dufournel

Arnaud Dufournel is the Coordinator of the Centre de la Francophonie at UBC. He is a Transportation Engineer and Geographer, who worked extensively on migration and the notion of equity in mobility.
